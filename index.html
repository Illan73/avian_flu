A detailed overview of avian influenza's virology, transmission, and public health risks

About Avian Influenza

Avian influenza (also known as bird flu) is an infectious disease caused by viruses that primarily infect birds, but certain strains can also infect humans and other animals. The disease is caused by influenza viruses that belong to the Orthomyxoviridae family and Influenza A virus genus. Avian influenza viruses are classified into two main groups: low pathogenic (LPAI) and highly pathogenic (HPAI), based on their ability to cause disease in birds.

1. Pathogen

  • Family: Orthomyxoviridae
  • Genus: Influenza A
  • Subtypes: H5N1, H7N9, H5N8

2. Transmission

  • Hosts: Wild waterfowl and domestic poultry
  • Transmission to Birds: Contact with infected birds or environments
  • Transmission to Humans: Direct contact with poultry or environments

3. Symptoms

In Birds: LPAI - mild or no symptoms; HPAI - severe, high mortality

In Humans: Fever, cough, pneumonia, ARDS, multi-organ failure (rare)

4. High-Risk Strains

  • H5N1: High mortality, human infections
  • H7N9: Emerged in China, severe human illness
  • H5N8: Major bird outbreaks, rare in humans

5. Pandemic Risk

Mutation and genetic reassortment can lead to strains capable of efficient human transmission.

6. Global Surveillance and Control

  • Surveillance by WHO, FAO
  • Poultry vaccines; experimental human vaccines
  • Biosecurity, quarantines, trade restrictions

7. Zoonotic & Evolutionary Concerns

Zoonotic spillover and viral evolution pose risks of a future human-adapted strain.

8. Climate Change Impact

Shifting bird migration and habitat due to climate change increases spread and zoonotic risk.

9. Key Points to Remember

  • Human transmission is rare but could evolve
  • HPAI strains (H5N1, H7N9) pose greatest risk
  • Surveillance and prevention are critical

Conclusion

Avian influenza remains a major zoonotic and public health concern. Ongoing research, monitoring, and prevention are crucial in mitigating its pandemic potential.
